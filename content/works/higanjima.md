There are two kinds of horror manga. One works on you from the inside — dread, implication, the thing you never quite see. *Higanjima* is the other kind, and it is not trying to be subtle, and it does not apologize for that.

An island cut off from the mainland. Vampires — not the brooding kind, the *mob* kind. And a group of ordinary young men who arrived looking for someone and now simply cannot leave. The fear here is not atmospheric. It is arithmetic. There are more of them than there are of you, the island will not let you go, and the people standing next to you are being removed from the equation chapter by chapter.

What keeps this from being disposable is that underneath the B-movie momentum — the gore, the increasingly desperate improvisations, the bodies — there is a specific, almost physical feeling of a closing perimeter. The island gets smaller as the series goes on. Not in pages. In the way it feels to be inside it.

Skip this if you are looking for psychological interiority or moral complexity. The characters are not here to be studied. They are here to endure. That is the whole question the manga keeps asking: *what does endurance actually look like when the odds are that bad.*

It is loud horror. But it is honest about what it is, and on the right night, honest loud horror is exactly what you need.
