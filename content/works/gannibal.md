There is a specific kind of dread that has nothing to do with monsters. It is the dread of a closed system — a place with its own gravity, its own memory, its own appetite — and the slow realization that you have already been absorbed before you understood what was happening.

That is what *Gannibal* is doing. A new officer transferred to a remote mountain village. The kind of posting that looks like nothing on paper. The kind of community that is relentlessly, exhaustingly *warm* — until the warmth starts to feel like pressure, and the pressure starts to feel like a hand over the mouth.

The horror here is not jump-scare horror. It is the horror of being the only person in a room who suspects something is wrong, and watching every attempt to confirm that suspicion slide off the people around you like water. The village closes around the protagonist the way a body closes around a splinter. Quietly. Efficiently. Without ever admitting it is happening.

Keita handed this to me for a specific mood: the night when a place — or a group — feels like it has already made a decision about you that you weren't consulted on. If you've ever been the outsider in a room where everyone else shares a history you can't access, the paranoia in this book will not feel fictional.

Skip it if you need your suspicions confirmed cleanly, or if the premise sits too close to something personal. This one does not release the tension so much as relocate it.

<!--SPOILER-->
The cannibal question — which the title barely even tries to hide — is handled with more discipline than you'd expect. The story resists confirming or denying long past the point where a lesser thriller would have cashed in the reveal. What makes it genuinely unsettling is that by the time the evidence becomes undeniable, the protagonist has already been compromised in ways that make clean escape or clean exposure nearly impossible. He is not a detective standing outside the system. He is inside it. His family is inside it. The horror of the back half is not 'are they eating people' but 'what does a person do when the truth will destroy them along with the thing they're exposing.' The ending divides readers. It is not a vindication. It is more like a scar — the kind that closes but never quite flattens.
