There is a particular kind of dread that Oshimi Shuzo understands better than almost anyone working in manga right now — not the dread of what is coming, but the dread of what has *already been happening for years* and you only just now have a name for it. 血の轍 is built entirely out of that feeling.

The surface is a quiet suburban family. A gentle, devoted mother. A boy who has never had reason to question how much she loves him. The horror is not that the surface cracks. It is that you begin to see, slowly, that the surface *is* the horror — that devotion and possession have been occupying the same space since before the first page.

Oshimi draws faces the way anxiety actually works: too still, too soft, held just a half-second too long. The unease arrives before you can point to anything wrong. That is precisely the point.

This is not a book about abuse in the way that word gets used clinically. It is about the profound confusion of a person who was loved by the thing that damaged him — and who cannot separate the two. Seventeen volumes of a boy trying to understand what is real inside a relationship that was never going to let him.

Skip this if you want the story to eventually take his side cleanly. It will not. Skip it if resolution feels like a requirement. This book is interested in the wound staying open, because that is what some wounds do.

<!--SPOILER-->
The incident at the cliff is the hinge the entire series turns on, and what makes it unbearable is not the act itself but Seichi's immediate, involuntary instinct to protect her. He does not hesitate. He does not understand why he does not hesitate. That reflex — protect the person who just did the unthinkable — is the thesis of the whole book made physical. Everything that follows is Seichi trying to locate himself inside a loyalty he never chose and cannot undo. The memory fragmentation Oshimi uses in the later volumes is not a stylistic flourish; it is the only honest way to render what dissociation inside a codependent bond actually looks like. The ending will frustrate readers who wanted Seichi free. What it offers instead is something closer to true: a self that survived, partial and unresolved, which is more than some people get.
