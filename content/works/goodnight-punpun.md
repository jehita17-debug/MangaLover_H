There is no monster in this one. That is what makes it hard to put down and harder to recover from. It moves at the speed of an actual life — slow, then all at once — and it refuses to tell you who to blame. You keep waiting for the book to take a side, to point at the parent or the friend or the world, and it never does. It just watches a person become the sum of his smallest avoidances.

Read this on the kind of night when you have stopped expecting to be special and have not yet made peace with that. It is not here to comfort you and it is not here to wound you for effect. It is interested in the long middle distance of a life, the part no one writes about, where nothing dramatic happens and everything is decided anyway.

Skip it if you need a character to be redeemed by the final page. Skip it if you read to escape rather than to sit with something. But if you have ever caught yourself being quietly disappointed by your own choices and wanted a book that understood that without flinching — this one will not lead you wrong.

<!--SPOILER-->
The genius of the ending is that the catastrophe everyone braces for arrives, is survived, and then *life simply continues* — which is somehow worse and truer than any tragedy. The narrative voice's final shift, away from the abstraction we have followed the whole way, lands as both an act of mercy and a quiet sentence. It earns its smallness.
