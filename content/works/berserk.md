The armor and the demons are the easy part to describe and the least of why it stays with you. Underneath the spectacle, this is a book about powerlessness, and about the specific damage done to a person who decides, early and absolutely, that he will never be at anyone's mercy again. The fights are extraordinary, but they are not the point. The point is the cost of that decision, paid slowly, over years, by the few people who manage to get close.

Read it on the kind of night when ambition has started to look frightening to you — your own, or someone else's. It is unsparing about the way a dream can become a justification for anything, and it is just as unsparing about the wreckage left in someone who survives what should have killed them. The horror here is rendered with real craft, but it never feels like decoration; every monstrous thing has a human shape underneath it.

Skip it if extreme violence or sexual brutality is something you cannot read past — this is genuinely dark, and it does not soften early. But if you want a long, serious work that treats trauma and survival and the seduction of power as worth a thousand pages, and that draws like almost nothing else in the medium, this one will not lead you wrong.

<!--SPOILER-->
The Eclipse is the hinge the whole series turns on, and what makes it unbearable is not the slaughter but the choice that precedes it: a friend deciding, with full understanding, that everyone who loves him is an acceptable price for what he wants to become. Everything before it reads differently once you know, and everything after is the long, grinding work of a survivor learning that revenge is not the same thing as healing — and may be its opposite.
