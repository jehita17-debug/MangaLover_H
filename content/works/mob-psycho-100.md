The fights are almost beside the point. ONE wraps this in the skin of an action comedy — espers, con artists, explosive set pieces — but what it is actually doing is quieter and stranger: asking whether power has anything to do with worth. The protagonist, Mob, could level a city block. He has decided, mostly, not to. That decision, held again and again under pressure, is where the story lives.

What stays with you is not the moments of release but the restraint leading up to them. The percentage counter ticking upward. The way he keeps choosing ordinary — school, friends, the slow humiliation of being unremarkable — over the thing that would make him immediately legible to the world. ONE seems genuinely unimpressed by strength as a value, and that skepticism runs all the way down.

This is not a story interested in whether Mob can win. It is interested in whether he can grow up without his power becoming his personality. That is a lonelier question than it sounds.

Skip it if you need your protagonist to want things loudly. Skip it if you need the power fantasy to be aspirational. Come to it on a night when you are tired of being defined by the one thing you are good at.
