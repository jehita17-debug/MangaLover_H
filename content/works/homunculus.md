There is a procedure at the center of this book — a trepanation, a hole drilled in the skull — and the horror is not the hole itself but what the protagonist starts seeing through it. Not monsters. People. Rendered as what they actually carry rather than what they show. *Homunculus* is about perception as wound. It asks, quietly and without mercy, whether clarity about others is insight or projection, whether knowing someone's hidden shape means you understand them or means you've simply found a more elaborate way to avoid yourself.

The dread here is not atmospheric. It is structural. It lives in the gap between what the main character sees and what we can verify. Yamamoto Hideo gives you no stable ground. The visual language — figures warped, bodies misaligned — is not stylistic flourish. It is the argument.

Read this if you want a book that makes you question the story you've been telling about your own interior life. Skip it if you need the protagonist to be trustworthy, or if body-image distortion is a subject you're carrying somewhere tender right now. It does not handle that material gently. It is not trying to.

<!--SPOILER-->
The question the book spends its entire length avoiding answering directly — and which makes it worth a second read — is whether Nakoshi's perceptions ever correspond to anything real about his subjects, or whether every 'homunculus' he sees is a confession about himself. The ending does not resolve this. What it does is collapse the distinction. By the final chapters, the trepanation has become a metaphor that has eaten its own vehicle: the hole in his head is no longer about seeing others clearly, it is about the specific shape of the self-narrative he built to survive becoming nobody. The figure he keeps seeing is his own. That's not a twist. It's a slow suffocation you feel coming from about volume four.
